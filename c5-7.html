Eid al-Adha

Eid al-Adha, known as the Festival of Sacrifice, is one of the two major Islamic holidays celebrated by Muslims worldwide, including in the Philippines. It occurs on the 10th day of Dhul-Hijjah, the last month of the Islamic lunar calendar, and marks the culmination of the Hajj pilgrimage to Mecca. Eid al-Adha commemorates the willingness of the Prophet Ibrahim (Abraham) to sacrifice his son Isma'il (Ishmael) in obedience to God's command. However, before the sacrifice could take place, God intervened and provided a ram to be sacrificed in place of Isma'il. This act of faith and devotion is central to the holiday’s meaning.

In the Philippines, Eid al-Adha is an important religious observance, especially for Muslims in Mindanao and other regions with significant Muslim populations. The holiday is a time for reflecting on the themes of obedience, sacrifice, and submission to the will of God. Eid al-Adha also emphasizes the spirit of charity and community, as Muslims are encouraged to share their blessings with others, particularly those in need.

What Do People Do?

On Eid al-Adha, Filipino Muslims begin the day by attending the Eid prayer, which is typically held in mosques or open spaces. This communal prayer is followed by a sermon and is a time for Muslims to gather with family, friends, and the wider community to give thanks to Allah for His blessings. After the prayer, Muslims who are financially able are encouraged to sacrifice an animal, typically a sheep, goat, cow, or camel, as a symbol of Ibrahim's willingness to sacrifice his son. The meat from the sacrifice is divided into three parts: one part is given to the needy, another part is shared with family and friends, and the remaining portion is kept for the family.

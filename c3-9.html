The Day of Valor

The Day of Valor, or Araw ng Kagitingan, is a national holiday in the Philippines observed annually on April 9. It honors the bravery and sacrifices of Filipino and American soldiers during World War II, particularly their endurance in the infamous Bataan Death March. This event followed the surrender of Bataan to Japanese forces on April 9, 1942, after months of intense fighting. Over 70,000 Filipino and American troops were forced to march approximately 65 miles under brutal conditions, leading to thousands of deaths due to exhaustion, starvation, and violence.

The holiday is a tribute to the courage and resilience of those who fought for the country’s freedom and a reminder of the sacrifices made for future generations. Initially called "Bataan Day," the observance was later renamed "Day of Valor" to encompass the broader heroism displayed across the nation during World War II. It serves as a significant occasion for reflecting on the country’s history and the enduring spirit of the Filipino people.

What Do People Do?

On the Day of Valor, Filipinos pay homage to the heroes of Bataan and World War II through various commemorative activities. Ceremonies are held at historical sites, such as the Mt. Samat National Shrine in Bataan, where government officials, veterans, and families gather to honor the fallen soldiers. Flags are flown at half-mast, and wreath-laying ceremonies take place to symbolize respect and remembrance. Schools and media platforms revisit the stories of wartime heroism, fostering patriotism among the younger generation. Many Filipinos use the day to reflect on the sacrifices of their ancestors and to express gratitude for the freedoms they enjoy today.

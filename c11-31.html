New Year's Eve

New Year's Eve, celebrated on December 31, marks the final day of the year in the Gregorian calendar and serves as a transition to the new year. It is a time of reflection on the past year, as well as a moment of celebration and hope for the year ahead. While the event is widely celebrated across the globe, New Year’s Eve holds special significance in the Philippines, where it is marked by various customs and traditions that are believed to bring good luck, prosperity, and happiness for the coming year.

In the Philippines, New Year's Eve is deeply intertwined with cultural practices, many of which are rooted in superstition and symbolism. Filipinos believe that how the year ends can affect how the new year begins. As such, people engage in different customs and festivities to ensure a prosperous and happy start to the year.

What Do People Do?

On New Year's Eve, Filipinos celebrate with grand festivities that often start in the evening and last until after midnight. Families gather for a special dinner called the Media Noche, which includes a variety of foods, such as pancit (noodles for long life), lechon (roast pig), and fruit salads, as well as round fruits that symbolize prosperity. The gathering is often accompanied by laughter, music, and merrymaking, as it is a time for family bonding and celebration.

At midnight, fireworks and firecrackers light up the sky as part of the Filipino tradition to drive away bad spirits and usher in good fortune. Loud noises, such as banging pots and pans or using horns, are also common to welcome the new year with a bang.

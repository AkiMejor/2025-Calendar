Bonifacio Day

Bonifacio Day, celebrated annually on November 30, honors the life and legacy of Andres Bonifacio, a Filipino revolutionary leader and one of the key figures in the Philippine struggle for independence. Bonifacio is best known for founding the Katipunan, a secret society that sought to overthrow Spanish colonial rule and establish an independent Philippines. His leadership and dedication to the cause of freedom made him one of the most revered heroes in the country. Although he did not live to see the country's independence, Bonifacio’s revolutionary efforts were pivotal in the eventual Philippine revolution against Spain.

Bonifacio Day was first celebrated in 1911, a few years after the Philippines gained independence, and it is recognized as a national holiday in honor of his contributions to the Filipino nation. His heroism, courage, and ultimate sacrifice in the fight for freedom continue to inspire generations of Filipinos to uphold the values of nationalism, justice, and independence.

What Do People Do?

On Bonifacio Day, Filipinos participate in various activities to commemorate the hero’s contributions to the country. Flag-raising ceremonies are held at key locations such as Bonifacio Monument in Caloocan, where government officials, educators, and citizens gather to pay tribute to Bonifacio. The ceremonies often include speeches highlighting Bonifacio’s role in the Philippine revolution, along with patriotic songs and cultural performances.

In schools and communities, discussions, programs, and exhibitions are organized to educate people about Bonifacio’s life and the significance of his revolutionary actions. Some regions also hold parades and public events celebrating Filipino heroism and nationalism. For many Filipinos, Bonifacio Day is an opportunity for reflection on the nation’s history and the enduring fight for freedom, unity, and justice. It is a day to honor not only Bonifacio but also the countless Filipinos who contributed to the country’s independence.
